# Bourbon vs Scotch: Key Differences

The article explains how bourbon and Scotch differ in history, geography, mash bill, fermentation, distillation, cask usage, maturation conditions, and legal definitions. It highlights that bourbon is primarily corn‑based, aged in new oak barrels, and often sweeter, while Scotch relies on malted barley, can use used or seasoned casks, and tends to be more complex with occasional peatiness.

## Key Points
- **History & Geography**: Bourbon originated in Kentucky; Scotch must be made in Scotland.
- **Mash Bill**: Bourbon ≥51 % corn (plus optional wheat/rye/barley); Scotch uses malted barley (100 % for single malts).
- **Fermentation**: Bourbon often uses sour mash/backset; Scotch ferments a wort to wash.
- **Distillation**: Scotch single malts use pot stills; grain whiskies use column stills. Bourbon typically uses a column first, then a pot.
- **Casks & Maturation**: Bourbon requires new oak for straight bourbon; Scotch can use new, seasoned, or refill casks. Scotland’s cooler climate reduces ABV over time; Kentucky’s heat increases it.
- **Legal Definitions**: U.S. law mandates specific mash, distillation, and aging criteria for bourbon; UK law sets similar limits for Scotch.
