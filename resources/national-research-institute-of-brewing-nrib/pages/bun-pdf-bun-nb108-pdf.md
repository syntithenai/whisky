# Summary of NRIB Document "108 原料利用種類"

This PDF (https://www.nrib.go.jp/bun/pdf/bun/nb108.pdf) outlines a series of experimental procedures for measuring the composition of whisky‑related liquids. The document is divided into numbered sections (108‑1 to 108‑8), each describing a different analytical method:

* **108‑1** – Sample collection and handling guidelines.
* **108‑2** – Determination of water content by gravimetric analysis.
* **108‑3** – Measurement of alcohol content using a calibrated glass tube and temperature‑controlled heating.
* **108‑4** – Calculation of total solids (dry matter) as the remainder after subtracting water and alcohol.
* **108‑5** – Determination of whisky‑specific solids via colorimetric assays with various reagents (e.g., L‑sistine, carbazole). Two sub‑methods (A & B) describe different reagent preparations.
* **108‑6** – Quantification of dissolved solids using a conductivity meter and calibration curves.
* **108‑7** – Analysis of whisky‑specific solids by spectrophotometry at 340 nm with multiple reaction steps.
* **108‑8** – Determination of viscosity (thickness) through a rotational viscometer, including temperature corrections.

Each section provides detailed reagent concentrations, volumes, temperatures, and calculation formulas. The document is intended for researchers in whisky chemistry and quality control.

## Key Takeaways
* Water content is measured gravimetrically after drying at 90 °C under 2.7 kPa pressure.
* Alcohol content is obtained by heating a calibrated tube to 70–75 °C, measuring the volume of vapor collected in a 6.7 kPa environment.
* Total solids are calculated as \(100\% - 	ext{water} - 	ext{alcohol}\).
* Whisky‑specific solids are quantified using colorimetric reactions with L‑sistine and carbazole, followed by spectrophotometric readings at 340 nm.
* Dissolved solids are measured via conductivity; whisky‑specific solids are derived from the difference between total dissolved solids and a standard solution.
* Viscosity is determined at various temperatures (20–125 °C) using a rotational viscometer, with empirical temperature corrections provided.

## Practical Implications
These methods provide a standardized framework for whisky laboratories to assess key quality attributes—water, alcohol, solids, and viscosity—ensuring consistency across batches and compliance with regulatory standards.
