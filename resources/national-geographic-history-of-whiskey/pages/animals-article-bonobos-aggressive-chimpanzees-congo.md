# Peace‑loving bonobos are more aggressive than we thought
National Geographic reports that male bonobos in the Democratic Republic of Congo display aggression—chasing, hitting, kicking and biting—more frequently than chimpanzee males, yet they rarely kill each other. The study, published in *Current Biology*, tracked 2,047 hours of bonobo behavior and recorded 521 aggressive acts, compared with 7,300 hours and 654 acts for chimpanzees in Gombe Stream National Park. Researchers suggest that lower costs of aggression and different territorial strategies allow bonobos to use force more readily, while female dominance and maternal support influence male reproductive success.

## Key findings
- Bonobo males exhibit higher rates of aggression than chimpanzee males.
- Aggression is less lethal in bonobos; no killings reported.
- Male bonobos with high‑ranking mothers or who are aggressive father more offspring.
- Female bonobos dominate and choose partners, reducing male coercion.

## Implications
The results challenge the stereotype of bonobos as entirely peaceful and highlight complex social dynamics that balance aggression, cooperation, and reproductive strategy.
