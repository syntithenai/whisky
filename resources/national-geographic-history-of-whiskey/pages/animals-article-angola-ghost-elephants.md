# Searching for Angola’s Ghost Elephants

National Geographic reports on a decade‑long quest to locate the elusive “ghost” elephants of Angola’s eastern highlands. The article details fieldwork by local hunter Abraão António Luhoke, ecologist Steve Boyes, and community leader Kerllen Costa, who together track footprints, broken saplings, camera‑trap footage, and even elephant dung to confirm the herd’s presence. It contextualises the elephants’ behaviour—nocturnal, silent, highly wary of humans—as a survival adaptation forged by decades of war and land‑mine devastation. The piece also explores cultural beliefs that view these animals as ancestral spirits, discusses conservation plans for local rangers, and highlights genetic studies underway with Stanford and Chicago universities.

## Key Points
- **Ghost elephants** live in highlands (~4 000 ft) and are rarely seen due to war‑induced fear of humans.
- Field team used **footprints, broken trees, camera traps, and dung samples** to locate the herd.
- Conservation goal: train up to 400 local rangers and restore human–elephant coexistence.
- Cultural context: locals believe elephants are ancestors in disguise; rituals involve traditional leaders.
- Genetic analysis will compare DNA from dung with known savanna/forest populations.
