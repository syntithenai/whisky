# The surprising hidden glow of one of Earth’s largest birds
Scientists discovered that the casque (horn‑like headgear) of southern cassowaries fluoresces under ultraviolet light, a phenomenon previously undocumented in these large flightless birds. The study, led by Todd L. Green and published in *Scientific Reports*, shows that both preserved museum specimens and living individuals emit bright blue‑green fluorescence when illuminated with UV wavelengths between 365–385 nm. This glow may serve as a visual signal helping cassowaries distinguish species or communicate within their rainforest habitat.

The research involved scanning over 100‑year‑old museum casques, then testing live birds such as Ginger from the Cassowary Conservation Project in Florida. Results indicate that different cassowary species display distinct fluorescence patterns, suggesting a potential role in visual display rather than defense. Future work aims to confirm visibility under natural conditions and explore conservation applications.

*Key points:* 1) First evidence of UV fluorescence in cassowary casques. 2) Fluorescence varies by species and casque region. 3) Possible function in species recognition or communication. 4) Opens new avenues for museum cataloging and conservation.
