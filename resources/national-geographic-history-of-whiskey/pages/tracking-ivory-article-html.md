# How Killing Elephants Finances Terror in Africa

National Geographic’s investigative piece explores how the illegal ivory trade fuels armed groups, especially the Lord’s Resistance Army (LRA), across Central and East Africa. The story follows a journalist who collaborates with taxidermist George Dante to create realistic fake elephant tusks embedded with GPS trackers. These “artificial” tusks are intended to trace the movement of poached ivory from protected parks—such as Garamba National Park in the Democratic Republic of Congo—to markets and ports, revealing the supply chain that supports terrorism.

Key points:
- **Ivory as currency**: Poachers sell ivory for cash or weapons; LRA leaders use it to finance operations.
- **Tracking strategy**: GPS‑enabled fake tusks are planted in poaching hotspots to monitor routes and destinations.
- **Geopolitical context**: The article details the historical ties between Sudan, Uganda, CAR, and DRC, illustrating how weak governance and poverty drive elephant killings.
- **Human toll**: Victims of LRA abductions, child soldiers, and community displacement are highlighted through interviews with local leaders and former fighters.
- **Legal and enforcement challenges**: The piece recounts a high‑profile airport incident in Tanzania where the fake tusks were mistaken for contraband, underscoring the complexities of wildlife law enforcement.

The narrative blends investigative journalism, on‑the‑ground reporting, and technical insights into GPS tracking to shed light on an under‑reported link between wildlife crime and terrorism.
