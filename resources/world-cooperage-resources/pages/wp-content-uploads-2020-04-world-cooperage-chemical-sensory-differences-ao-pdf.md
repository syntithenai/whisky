# Chemical & Sensory Differences in American Oak Toasting Profiles
This World Cooperage study compared 18 different American oak toasting profiles (plus a control) using Cabernet Sauvignon wine from 2005. Four barrels per profile were aged ~10 months, then bottled together. The experiment measured 27 oak extractives and selected wine phenolics, performed sensory evaluation for vanilla, smoke, fruit, toast, spice, and overall preference, and applied statistical analyses (ANOVA, LSD, PLS, PCA).

**Key findings**
- Profiles toasted above 400 °F for extended periods produced higher smoke perception; those below 400 °F yielded more fruit character.
- Preference correlated positively with fruit and negatively with smoke.
- Vanillin levels were sufficient to influence wine flavor across all profiles.
- Two distinct chemical clusters emerged: one dominated by oak lactones & smoke phenolics, the other by toast‑related compounds (HMF, furfural) and spice notes.

The report includes detailed tables of compound concentrations, statistical groupings, and graphical representations of toasting curves and multivariate analyses.
