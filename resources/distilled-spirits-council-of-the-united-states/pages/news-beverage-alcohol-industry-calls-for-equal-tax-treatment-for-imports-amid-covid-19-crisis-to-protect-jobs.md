# Beverage Alcohol Industry Calls for Equal Tax Treatment for Imports Amid COVID‑19 Crisis

The Distilled Spirits Council of the United States (DSCU) and other U.S. beverage alcohol associations sent a letter to Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin on April 23, 2020, urging that federal excise tax deferral policies be applied equally to domestic producers and importers. While the Alcohol‑Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau allowed all U.S. distillers, brewers and vintners a 90‑day deferment of excise taxes, Customs and Border Protection (CBP) required importers to prove a >40 % revenue loss before granting a similar deferment. The letter requests CBP align its guidance with the Alcohol‑Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau’s policy, extending the deferral through July 1, 2020.

The DSCU highlights that the beverage alcohol industry supports millions of jobs and billions in wages, taxes, and benefits—making equitable financial relief critical during the pandemic.

---

**Key points:**
- Letter signed by DSCU, American Distilled Spirits Alliance, National Association of Beverage Importers, Beer Institute, Wine & Spirits Wholesalers of America, and Wine and Spirits Shippers Association.
- CBP’s current deferment requires proof of >40 % revenue loss; domestic producers receive a blanket 90‑day deferment.
- The letter calls for parity in deferral policy and extension through July 1, 2020.

## Source Excerpts
- beverage alcohol associations urged the Administration to provide parity in the deferral of federal excise taxes payment for domestic and imported wine, beer and distilled spirits in response to the outbreak of COVID-19.
- On Monday, Customs and Border Protection issued guidance for beverage alcohol importers allowing them to delay their federal excise tax payments only if they prove they have faced a more than 40 percent loss of revenue.
- This onerous requirement and disparate guidance will mean importers—which support thousands of jobs in the United States—will not have the capital that is critical for all businesses to protect jobs.
- The letter requests Customs of Border Protection bring their policy in line with the Alcohol Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau to provide a 90-deferral for beer, wine and spirit importers to pay for federal excise taxes.
- In the letter, the associations applauded the Administration’s recent efforts to provide financial relief to affected industries during the unprecedented outbreak of COVID-19 but requested equal tax treatment for importers.
- “Importers of beer, wine, and spirits pay the same federal excise tax as domestic producers,” said the industry leaders.
