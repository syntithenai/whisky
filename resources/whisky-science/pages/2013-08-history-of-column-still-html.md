# History of the Column Still
The article traces the evolution of column (continuous) stills from early 11th‑century pot stills to the modern Coffey design, highlighting key inventors (Adam, Pistorius, Cellier‑Blumenthal, Savalle, Coffey) and their patents. It explains how continuous distillation improved efficiency, reduced costs, and enabled grain whisky production that later dominated commercial blends in Britain and Ireland.

Key points:
- Early stills were simple pot designs; water‑jacketed stills appeared 1526.
- The first column still (Adam 1801) used horizontal chambers; Pistorius refined it with vertical plates.
- Cellier‑Blumenthal’s 1813 patent combined multiple rectifying plates and steam heating, leading to the modern Coffey still.
- Coffey’s design (1830s) introduced perforated copper plates and a two‑column system, becoming standard for grain whisky.
- Column stills produced high‑proof spirits at lower cost, shifting distilling economics in the 19th century.

The post also lists early adopters of Coffey stills in Scotland and Ireland and discusses the rise of blended whisky markets.
