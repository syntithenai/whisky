# Sugars in Whisky

The article explains that whisky contains only trace amounts of sugars – usually below **1 g L⁻¹** – originating mainly from the oak cask and, occasionally, from caramel colouring (E150a). It details how different cask histories (bourbon, sherry, rum, etc.) influence sugar extraction, the role of wood chemistry, alcohol strength, and the legal limits on additives. The piece also reviews historical practices such as sherry seasoning and Spanish cooperage, citing studies that quantify sugar levels in various spirits.

## Key Points
- **Typical sugar content**: <1 g L⁻¹; can reach a few g L⁻¹ with sweet‑wine casks.
- **Sources**: oak (cellulose, hemicellulose), caramel colouring, previous spirit or wine in the cask.
- **Legal limits**: no sweeteners allowed in Scotch/Irish whisky; E150a is permitted but usually <1 g L⁻¹.
- **Extraction factors**: wood type, toasting level, alcohol strength, and prior use (fermentation, sherry, rum).
- **Historical context**: 19th‑20th century use of Spanish shipping casks for sherry seasoning; modern practices favor dry sherries.
