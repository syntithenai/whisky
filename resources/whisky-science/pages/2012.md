# Whisky Science – 2012

- **Triple distillation**: Auchentoshan and Hazelburn (Springbank) use three‑fold pot stills; Benrinnes, Springbank, Mortlach employ partial or full quadruple systems. Irish distilleries historically used triple distillation to achieve cleaner spirits.
- **Lactic acid bacteria (LAB)**: Present in whisky fermentation, influencing flavour through lactic/acetic acids and lactones. LAB growth depends on distillery hygiene and fermentation time.
- The page contains detailed historical notes, technical descriptions of still configurations, and references for further reading.

---

## Key facts
- Auchentoshan’s triple‑distillation uses a narrow heart cut (82.5–80 % ABV). 
- Mortlach’s “Wee Witchie” small still performs three distillations on weak feints, contributing to its meaty aroma.
- Irish pot stills in the 19th century adopted triple distillation for malt‑grain blends.
- LAB species such as *L. fermentum*, *Pediococcus* spp., and *Streptococcus* spp. are common; they can raise lactic acid, acetic acid, and lactones.
- Fermentation times vary by distillery; longer runs (48–70 h) increase LAB activity.

---

## Distillery facts
- Auchentoshan – triple‑distilled pot stills, light & aromatic whisky. 
- Hazelburn – operated 1837‑1925, used water‑jacketed tube condensers; revived in 1997 at Springbank with normal stills.
- Benrinnes – partial triple distillation since ~1954; uses a low‑wine still for weak feints.
- Springbank – 1/5 wash double distilled, 4/5 triple distilled; all feints added to intermediate still.
- Mortlach – 80 % double distilled, 20 % quadruple distilled via “Wee Witchie” small still.
