# Whisky Science – 2015

- **Pot‑still variations**: Scottish distilleries use a wide range of pot stills (e.g., Glenkinchie 30 l, Edradour 2 l, Glenmorangie 8 m tall). Shapes vary from onion‑shaped to tear‑shaped and slender. Many have reflux balls or boil‑balls that influence ester formation.
- **Historical context**: Distillation in Scotland evolved under taxation pressures (e.g., 1725 malt tax, 1784 still limits). Illicit distilling drove small “sma’” stills; later legalisation allowed larger stills and steam heating.
- **Heating methods**: Direct‑fire was common until the late 19th c.; indirect steam coils became widespread in the mid‑20th c. Some distilleries (Glenfiddich, Macallan) still use direct fire.
- **Reflux & purification**: Boil balls, water‑cooled heads, purifiers and worm tubs are used to increase reflux or remove heavier congeners. The Lomond still (reflux column inside the pot) was first commercialised in Scotland in the 1930s.
- **Barley mash history**: Two‑row barley dominated Scottish malt; six‑row varieties were mainly cattle feed. Over time, foreign barleys (Danish, Chilean, Australian) entered the market, especially for grain whisky where maize became dominant.
