# Sugars in Whisky

The article explains that Scotch whisky contains only trace amounts of sugars – usually below 1 g L⁻¹ – originating mainly from the oak cask and, rarely, from caramel colouring (E150a). It details how different cask histories (ex‑bourbon, ex‑sherry, toasted or charred) influence sugar extraction, the role of E150a in flavour perception, and compares whisky sugars with those found in rums and sweet wines. The piece cites studies on sugar content, caramel composition, and the chemistry of oak components.

## Key points
- **Typical sugar level**: < 1 g L⁻¹; can reach a few g L⁻¹ if cask previously held sweet wine.
- **Sources**: Oak (cellulose, hemicellulose), previous cask contents, optional caramel colouring.
- **E150a**: Mostly water, carbohydrates and ash; 5‑HMF gives colour; legal additive in Scotch.
- **Cask influence**: Ex‑bourbon → minimal sugars; ex‑sherry (especially Pedro Ximénez) → higher sugars.
- **Extraction rates**: ~100–250 mg L⁻¹ yr⁻¹ for new charred oak; lower for old refill casks.
- **Alcohol strength matters**: Lower ABV extracts more sugars from wood.
- **Comparisons**: Rum (up to 40 g L⁻¹), sweet sherries (≈200–500 g L⁻¹).
