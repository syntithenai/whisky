# A Guide to the 3‑Chamber Still

- The 3‑chamber still is a semi‑continuous batch still that historically produced robust, aromatic spirits such as rye whiskey and rum.
- First popular in the mid‑to‑late 1800s, early models used wood; later versions were metalised for durability.
- Compared to double retort systems (larger primary kettle + two ancillary kettles), the 3‑chamber still offers longer dwell time, which enhances aromatics but is slower than column stills.
- The design was largely forgotten until Todd Leopold of Leopold Bros. revived it in the early 2020s, citing flavor as the key advantage.
- StillDragon has developed a modular, user‑friendly version of the 3‑chamber still and plans to showcase it at upcoming trade shows.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Long dwell time (up to ~90 min)** → richer aromatics.
2. **Semi‑continuous operation** – feeds beer continuously but retains batch characteristics.
3. **Historical context** – used by major distilleries like Hiram Walker for flavor, not speed.
4. **Modern revival** – Todd Leopold’s research and StillDragon’s modular design bring the technology back into production.
