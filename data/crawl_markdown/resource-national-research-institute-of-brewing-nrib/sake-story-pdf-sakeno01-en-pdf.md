# Sake Production Overview

- **Historical context**: Sake brewing dates back to the Yayoi period (300 BC‑250 AD) and became institutionalised during the Nara, Heian, Muromachi, and Edo periods.
- **Key processes**: Rice polishing, washing & soaking, steaming, koji inoculation, fermentation mash preparation, pressing, pasteurisation, ageing, bottling.
- **Important ingredients**: Special sake rice varieties (e.g., Yamadanishiki), koji mold (*Aspergillus oryzae*), yeast, water, optional distilled alcohol for flavour adjustment.
- **Scientific aspects**: Enzymatic conversion of starch to sugars by koji; yeast fermentation to alcohol; temperature and humidity control during koji‑making.
- **Institutional role**: The National Research Institute of Brewing (NRIB) was established in 1904 to advance scientific research on sake production.

---

## Key facts
- Sake is a rice‑based alcoholic beverage, integral to Japanese culture and cuisine.
- Rice polishing rates determine quality: *daiginjo* uses >70 % polishing; *junmai* uses 40–50 %.
- Koji mold produces enzymes that break down starch into fermentable sugars.
- Yeast converts sugars into alcohol and CO₂.
- Pasteurisation (heat sterilisation) preserves the final product.

---

## Production highlights
1. **Rice preparation** – wash, soak, steam, cool.
2. **Koji inoculation** – spread spores on steamed rice; maintain ~30 °C and high humidity for 48 h.
3. **Fermentation mash (moromi)** – mix koji‑rice with water, add yeast, ferment at controlled temperatures.
4. **Pressing & filtration** – separate liquid from solids.
5. **Pasteurisation & ageing** – heat‑sterilise and age for flavour development.
6. **Bottling** – final packaging.

---

## NRIB contribution
- Established in 1904 as a national research institute for alcoholic beverages.
- Conducts scientific studies on sake making and develops new technologies.

### Glossary
- **Toji**: chief sake maker.
- **Koji**: mold (*Aspergillus oryzae*) used to saccharify rice.
- **Moromi**: fermentation mash.
- **Daiginjo‑shu**: high‑quality sake with >70 % rice polishing.
