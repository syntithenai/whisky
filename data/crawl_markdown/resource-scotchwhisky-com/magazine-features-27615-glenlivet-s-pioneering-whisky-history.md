# Glenlivet’s Pioneering Whisky History

The article traces the evolution of the Glenlivet valley from an illicit cottage‑industry hub to a cornerstone of Speyside whisky culture. Key points include:
- Early distillers like William Gordon and George Smith operated without licences, smuggling whisky in small casks called *ankers*.
- The 1823 Excise Act and Duke of Gordon’s intervention forced many illegal stills into legal operation, but competition from larger southern distilleries led to closures.
- By the early 20th c., modern machinery was introduced; the Glenlivet style shifted from heavily peated to a richer, pineapple‑flavoured profile as demand changed.
- The name *Glenlivet* became generic for Highland pot‑still whisky until trademark disputes and the creation of the term *Speyside* clarified branding.
- Notable figures such as the Grants, Smiths, and later Bill Smith Grant helped establish the brand internationally, especially in the U.S. after Prohibition.
- The 1960s saw new distilleries (Tamnavulin, Braeval) built to supply blends; Tamnavulin closed in 1995 but reopened in 2007.

The piece underscores Glenlivet’s lasting influence on whisky production techniques, legal frameworks, and global branding.
