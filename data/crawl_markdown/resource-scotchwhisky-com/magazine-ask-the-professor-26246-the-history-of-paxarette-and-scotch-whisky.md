# The history of paxarette and Scotch whisky
The article explains that **paxarette** (often called *pax*) was a sweetening and colouring agent used in the 19th‑century Scotch whisky industry to rejuvenate sherry casks. It originated from *vino de color* produced near Jerez, Spain, and was added to sherry blends before fortification. The practice grew when sherry consumption fell in Scotland, creating a shortage of empty sherry butts; distillers used paxarette to restore colour and flavour in exhausted casks. In 1990 the Scotch Whisky Order banned paxarette because it altered whisky flavour.

- **Origin**: Spanish *vino de color* → paxarette.
- **Use**: Rejuvenate sherry casks, boost flavour of exhausted oak or ex‑solera casks.
- **Ban**: 1990 – considered an additive that changes flavour.
- **Health**: No health risk; ban was regulatory, not safety‑based.

The piece also notes that paxarette is still produced and used in Spain but no longer permitted in Scottish distilleries.
