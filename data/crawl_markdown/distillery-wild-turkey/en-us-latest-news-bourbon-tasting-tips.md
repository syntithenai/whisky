# Mastering the Art of Bourbon Tasting
Wild Turkey explains how to taste bourbon versus simply drinking it, outlining a step‑by‑step method that includes:
- Choosing the right glass (Scotch‑style or wine glass)
- Observing color and legs for age clues
- Sniffing, sipping, pausing, and using the retronasal technique
- Adding water to open flavors
- Noting finish (“Kentucky Hug”) and performing a “Kentucky Chew” for deeper perception
The article also lists common flavor notes (vanilla, chocolate, spice, fruit, smoke) and explains how grain composition and barrel aging create these aromas.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. **Tasting vs. drinking** – tasting focuses on identifying individual aromas and flavors; drinking is for enjoyment.
2. **Glass choice matters** – a wide‑balled glass funnels aromas to the nose.
3. **Color & legs indicate age/intensity** – darker, longer legs suggest more mature bourbon.
4. **Retronasal technique** – exhaling after swallowing releases back‑of‑mouth aromas.
5. **Water dilution** – a drop can reveal hidden flavors.
6. **Finish evaluation** – assess length, smoothness, and lingering notes.
7. **Kentucky Chew** – chewing bourbon with air in the mouth enhances palate coverage.

## Source Excerpts
- WHAT DOES BOURBON TASTE LIKE Through the magic of fermentation, distillation and maturation, bourbon develops into a tapestry of flavors.
- After distillation, the spirit goes into charred new oak barrels.
- Experts estimate that up to 70% of bourbon’s flavor comes from the barrel-aging process, particularly when a whiskey is aged for longer than the legally required minimum, as all Wild Turkey whiskeys are.
- In the whiskey business, the best days involve tasting bourbon that’s reached the peak of maturation.
- Sampling a bourbon neat, or with a splash of water, and really focusing on flavor—that’s where tasting comes in.
- The aim is to take note of individual aromas and identify flavors; to develop the ability to distinguish the upfront notes from the lingering finish and everything in between.
