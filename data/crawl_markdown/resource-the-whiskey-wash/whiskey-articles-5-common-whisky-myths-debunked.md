# 5 Common Whisky Myths Debunked

Mark Littler tackles five widespread whisky misconceptions:
1. **Older isn’t always better** – after a peak (10‑14 yrs for bourbon) extra aging adds tannin and can mask character.
2. **Single malt ≠ single barrel** – the term means one distillery, not one cask; most are blends of many barrels.
3. **Neat is mandatory** – adding 20‑30 % water releases aromatic compounds; traditions worldwide use mixers or water.
4. **Scotch is the only real whisky** – Irish, Japanese, Taiwanese and Australian whiskies have won world awards; global sales are dominated by India, US and Ireland.
5. **Darker = better** – colour can come from caramel additives (E150a) or cask type; pale whiskies can be exceptional.

The article cites experts (Eddie Russell, Jonathan Bray, Björn Karlsson), studies, and award winners to support each point.

## Source Excerpts
- Most single malts on the market are actually a marriage of many different barrels, carefully selected and blended to create a consistent flavor profile.
- In 2015, Yamazaki Sherry Cask from Japan was crowned “World Whisky of the Year” with Jim Murray praising it as “near indescribable genius.” Taiwan’s Kavalan won “World’s Best Single Malt” that same year, while Australia’s Sullivan’s Cove claimed the title in 2014.
- Taiwan’s Kavalan won ‘World’s Best Single Malt’ with a whisky reportedly just five years old, while Crown Royal Northern Harvest Rye (with no age statement) topped Jim Murray’s influential list in 2016.
- Myth 2: “Single Malt Means Single Barrel” Despite what many believe, a single malt whisky doesn’t come from just one barrel.
- This distinction matters because it highlights the blender’s craft – your favorite The Macallan or The Glenlivet isn’t the product of one magical cask, but rather a skillful symphony of many barrels working in harmony.
- Ex-bourbon casks typically impart golden hues, while ex-sherry casks give darker amber tones.
