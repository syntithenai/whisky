# Arctic Hybrid Bears and Other Species

National Geographic reports that climate change is increasing the likelihood of hybridization among Arctic species, most notably polar‑grizzly bear hybrids (often called *grolars* or *pizzlies*) and a beluga‑narwhal hybrid (*narluga*). Eight polar‑grizzly hybrids have been documented, all descending from a single female polar bear. Genetic studies show that hybridization remains rare but could rise as sea ice melts and species ranges shift northward.

The article also highlights other Arctic hybrids—beluga‑narwhals, Atlantic puffins, and even potential whale hybrids—and discusses the ecological implications of gene flow between species, including possible benefits (hybrid vigor) and risks (outbreeding depression, genetic swamping). Conservationists emphasize monitoring these populations to adapt management strategies as climate change continues.

## Key Points
- **Hybrid bear types**: *grolars* (grizzly father), *pizzlies* (polar father).
- **Genetic evidence**: Eight documented hybrids; all share a common polar‑bear ancestor.
- **Other hybrids**: Beluga‑narwhal (*narluga*), Atlantic puffin subspecies, whale gene flow.
- **Climate link**: Melting sea ice and shifting habitats increase contact between species.
- **Conservation concerns**: Hybridization may affect fitness, reproductive success, and legal protection status.
