# Excise Notice 39 - Spirits Production in the UK

## Key Facts
- Covers spirits production, including whisky, across England, Scotland and Wales
- Regulates excise duty on spirits produced domestically or imported into the UK
- Sets standards for alcohol strength, packaging, labeling and storage requirements
- Mandates registration of distilleries and licensed premises handling spirits
- Enforces compliance through inspections, audits and penalties for violations

## Production Signals
- Distilleries must adhere to strict production processes outlined in Excise Notice 39
- Alcohol content limits apply based on proof spirit strength (e.g., whisky at minimum 40% ABV)
- Packaging requirements mandate clear labeling of alcohol percentage, distillery name and age statements for certain classifications
- Maturation periods vary by category (e.g., blended whisky aged a minimum of three years)
- Quality control measures ensure consistency in flavor profiles across batches

## Commercial Signals
- Excise duty rates fluctuate based on prevailing market conditions and government fiscal policies
- Licensed premises must pay duties on spirits sold to consumers, with varying rates for different alcohol strengths
- Importers face import duties plus VAT on foreign-produced spirits entering the UK market
- Compliance with labeling regulations impacts marketing strategies and brand positioning

## Risks / Unknowns
- Changes in excise legislation could impact production costs and profit margins for distilleries
- Strict storage conditions pose logistical challenges, especially for aging warehouses
- Counterfeit spirits pose a risk to consumer safety and brand reputation
- Regulatory changes may necessitate updates to compliance procedures and documentation
