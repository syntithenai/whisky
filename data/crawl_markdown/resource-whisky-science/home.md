# Sugars in Whisky

## Key Facts
- Small amounts of sugar are dissolved from the oak cask and optional caramel colouring (E150a) in all whiskies.
- Total amount of sugars is usually well below 1 g/l, but can reach a few grams per liter in certain cases.
- New make spirit does not contain significant amounts of any sugars and it's illegal to add sweeteners to Scottish whisky, Irish whisky or bourbon.
- Caramel colouring is an allowed additive in Scotch whisky.

## Production Signals
- Oak composition: Cellulose (D-glucose fiber), Hemicellulose (xylose, arabinose, galactose, ribose, rhamnose) and Lignin (polypropane and polyphenols).
- Sugars extracted from oak come mostly from hemicellulose, hence most are 5-carbon sugars.
- Toasting and charring of the oak lowers extractable sugars, converting them to furfurals.
- High entry proof likely diminishes sugar extraction.

## Commercial Signals
- Most ex-bourbon casks impart minimal amounts of sugars (<50 mg/l/year).
- Charred American oak used for bourbon extracts a major part of available sugars.
- Toasted cask previously used for non-sugared spirits could extract slightly more, but still not over 1 g/l during refill maturation.
- Venezuelan rum can contain up to 40 g/l sugars; sweetened rums add sugar at bottling phase or in cask.
- Pedro Ximenez sherry contains over 212 g/l sugars and is a common cask for whisky seasoning.

## Risks/Unknowns
- Legal limits on caramel content are not strict, but 2g/l of E150a would render the whisky very dark.
- High proof spirits likely diminish sugar extraction due to lower solubility in ethanol-water solution.
