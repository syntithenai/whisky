# Whisky Science – History

Whisky Science is a blog that compiles technical and historical information about Scotch single‑malt whisky. The *history* tag gathers posts on the evolution of distillation equipment, taxation, and filtering practices from the 17th to the 21st century.

Key themes:
- **Pot still variations** – sizes, shapes (onion‑shaped, tear‑shaped, slender), and features such as boil balls, purifiers, and cooling systems.
- **Taxation & illicit distilling** – how excise duties shaped still size and the rise of “sma’” pan stills in the Highlands.
- **Heating methods** – transition from direct fire to steam jackets and coils; early adopters like Glenmorangie (1887) and later widespread use in the 1950‑60s.
- **Reflux experiments** – introduction of Lomond, Balvenie boil balls, and American‑style continuous still concepts.
- **Filtering & chill‑filtration** – historical practices from cloth to charcoal to modern plate‑and‑frame systems; impact on haze formation and flavour.

The posts are heavily footnoted with academic references and include many photographs of distillery equipment.

## Distillery Facts
- Glenmorangie stills are 8 m tall; Glenkinchie wash still is 30,963 L; Edradour spirit still is 2,179 L.
- Glenmorangie first used steam heating in 1887.
- Glenfiddich, Glenfarclas and Macallan use direct‑fire for both wash and spirit stills.
- Glen Grant uses purifiers on all its stills; Ardbeg uses uncooled purifiers.
- Littlemill introduced the first commercial Lomond reflux still in the early 1930s.
