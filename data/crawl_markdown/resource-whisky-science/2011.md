# Whisky Science – 2011

The page is a collection of detailed notes on whisky fermentation, yeast biology and flavour chemistry. It covers:
- The role of unboiled wort, aerobic yeast propagation and temperature control in Scotch single‑malt production.
- Yeast metabolism: lag, log, stationary and death phases; production of higher alcohols (fusel), esters, diacetyl, sulphur compounds and phenols.
- Tables of ester–odor relationships and a list of key flavour‑forming compounds.
- Discussion of yeast species/strains used in whisky (S. cerevisiae M‑strain, hybrids, ale vs lager strains) and their genetic background.
- Practical aspects such as flocculation, attenuation, sugar utilisation, alcohol tolerance and killer toxins.
- Historical notes on the development of pure‑culture distiller’s yeasts and references for further reading.

The page is largely technical and cites many academic sources.

---

**Key take‑aways:**
1. **Fermentation chemistry** – higher alcohols + acids → esters; diacetyl from amino‑acid metabolism; sulphur compounds from yeast and malt.
2. **Yeast strain choice** – M‑strain (S. cerevisiae hybrid) dominates Scotch; ale strains produce more fusel alcohols.
3. **Process variables** – temperature, nitrogen, pitching rate, flocculation affect flavour profile.
4. **Historical context** – pure‑culture yeasts emerged in the 1920s–30s; M‑strain introduced 1952.
