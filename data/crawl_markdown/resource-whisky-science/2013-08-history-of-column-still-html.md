# History of the Column Still
The article traces the evolution of column (continuous) stills from early 11th‑century pot stills to modern Coffey designs, highlighting key inventors (Paracelsus, Ulstadius, Weigel, Adam, Saluzzo, Cellier‑Blumenthal, Savalle, Stein, Coffey). It explains how the column still improved efficiency, produced higher‑proof spirits, and enabled grain whisky production. The text also lists early adopters in Britain, Ireland, France, and Scandinavia, and discusses economic impacts such as lower costs, price cartels, and market expansion.

---
**Key Points**
- Early distillation used pot stills; column stills emerged in the 19th c.
- Adam’s 1801 horizontal column was the first working design.
- Cellier‑Blumenthal (1808) patented a vertical continuous still with rectifying plates.
- Aeneas Coffey (1830) introduced copper perforated plates and later two‑column designs.
- Column stills produced 86–95 % ABV, ten times the volume of pot stills, at 50–70 % lower cost.
- Adoption spread through Scotland, Ireland, and Britain; grain whisky became dominant.
- Economic factors: cheaper production, blending, price cartels (DCL), tax reforms.
