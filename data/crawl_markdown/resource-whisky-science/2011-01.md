# Oak Species Used in Whisky Casks

This January 2011 post from *Whisky Science* provides a detailed overview of the oak species employed in whisky cooperage, focusing on their distribution, characteristics, and suitability for cask production. It covers American white oaks (e.g., *Quercus alba*, *Q. robur*, *Q. petraea*), European oaks, Asian varieties such as Japanese mizunara (*Q. crispula*), and historical uses of Persian oak. The article also discusses the influence of oak chemistry on flavour development and cites numerous scientific studies.

---

## Key Points
- **American white oaks** dominate Scotch whisky casks (≈ 97 %) due to high tyloses content, making them waterproof and imparting desirable flavours.
- **European oaks** (*Q. robur*, *Q. petraea*) are common in cooperage for wine and some whiskies; hybrids are frequent.
- **Japanese mizunara** (*Q. crispula* / *Q. dentata*) offers a unique sweet‑spicy profile but is harder to work with because of fewer tyloses.
- The article lists many other oak species used regionally (e.g., *Q. bicolor*, *Q. macrocappa*, *Q. garryana*, *Q. pyrenaica*).
- Scientific references are provided for readers who wish to explore the chemistry and genetics of cooperage woods.

---

## Practical Takeaway
When selecting casks, consider not only species but also provenance, age, and whether the wood has been toasted or charred, as these factors influence the final whisky flavour profile.
