# Whisky Science – February 2011

This blog post is a technical overview of how oak casks influence Scotch whisky maturation. It covers the types of wood used, the chemistry of extractives (lactones, tannins, phenols, furfurals, etc.), the impact of seasoning and toasting, and how different oak species (Quercus alba, Q. robur, Q. petraea, Q. crispula) contribute distinct flavour profiles. The article also discusses the role of first‑fill versus refill casks, the effect of spirit strength on extraction, and the importance of wood surface area and cask size.

Key points:
- All modern Scotch casks must be oak; earlier varieties included chestnut.
- First fills extract most soluble compounds; refills are drier but still impart tannins and phenolics.
- Oak lactones (cis‑ and trans‑) give coconut, vanilla and spicy notes; their ratio varies by species.
- Tannins, eugenol, vanillin, furfural, guaiacols, and other lignin derivatives are major contributors to aroma and colour.
- Seasoning, kilning, toasting, and charring alter extractive profiles and permeability.
- Cask size influences oxidation rate and extraction balance between sugars/glycerol and lignin‑derived compounds.

The post concludes with a table summarising species differences and an extensive reference list.
