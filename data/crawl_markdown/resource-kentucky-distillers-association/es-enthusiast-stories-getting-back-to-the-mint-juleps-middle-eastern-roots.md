# Getting Back to the Mint Julep’s Middle Eastern Roots

The article explains how the classic Kentucky Mint Julep can be flavored with *atar* (rose‑water simple syrup) inspired by Middle‑Eastern desserts like knafeh. It traces the word “julepe” back to Persian *gulab* and Arabic *julab*, describes the authors’ personal experience, gives a step‑by‑step recipe for rose‑water atar and an adapted mint julep, and briefly profiles the writers Janice W. Fernheimer and Jim Ridolfo.

---

## Key Points
- **Historical link**: *julepe* derives from Persian/Arabic terms for rose‑petal drinks.
- **Personal story**: Authors’ exposure to knafeh during a Fulbright in the Middle East inspired the recipe.
- **Atar recipe**: 1½ cups sugar, 1¼ cups cold water, ½ tsp lemon juice, 1 Tbsp rose‑water; simmer 12 min, add rose‑water, boil 30 s.
- **Mint julep**: ¼ oz atar, 8 mint leaves, 2–2.5 oz Kentucky bourbon, crushed ice, garnish with mint.
- **Authors**: Fernheimer – professor of writing & Jewish studies; Ridolfo – associate professor of writing & digital humanities.

---

## Recipe Highlights
- *Atar* can also be made with orange‑blossom water.
- Store atar in the fridge for up to a month.
- Use any Kentucky bourbon; garnish with fresh mint and an eco‑straw.

## About the Authors
- **Janice W. Fernheimer**: Writing, rhetoric & Jewish studies professor; author of *Entering Zion* (2014). 
- **Jim Ridolfo**: Associate writing professor; Fulbright scholar in Middle East/North Africa; author on digital humanities.
