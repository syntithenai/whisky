# Chemical and Sensory Differences in American Oak Toasting Profiles
World Cooperage conducted a controlled experiment on 18 American oak barrel profiles (plus a control) using the same wood lot, seasoning time, and wine batch (Kendall‑Jackson Cabernet Sauvignon 2005). Four barrels per profile were aged ~10 months. The study measured 27 oak extractives, selected wine phenolics, and performed sensory evaluation for vanilla, smoke, fruit, toast, spice, and overall preference.

**Key findings**
- Preference correlated positively with fruit perception and negatively with smoke perception.
- Toasting temperatures above **400 °F** for extended periods produced higher smoke character; lower temperatures yielded more fruit character.
- Two chemical clusters emerged: one dominated by lactones and smoke markers (cresols, guaiacol), the other by toasted oak markers (HMF, furfural) and spice compounds.
- Vanillin levels were similar across profiles but varied slightly within each cluster.
- The control (used 2002 barrels) had the highest fruit and lowest smoke scores, confirming that fresh toasting imparts distinct sensory attributes.

The report recommends using a control in all barrel experiments and highlights the ability of temperature‑time profiling to tailor barrels for specific wine styles or blending purposes.
