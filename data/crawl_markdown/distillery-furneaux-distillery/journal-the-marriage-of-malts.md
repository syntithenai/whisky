# The Marriage of Malts

Furneaux Distillery explores a unique peating technique called **post‑malting**: Tasmanian barley is first malted, then smoked with local Flinders peat before being blended with Scottish heavily peated malt. The result is a whisky that balances an earthy, warm back‑palate from the island peat with an immediate, dark roasted, sweet BBQ smoke from the Scottish peat.

The article explains peat formation, compares PPM levels for enthusiasts, and highlights the experimental nature of combining two distinct peat profiles.

---

**Key points**:
- Post‑malting vs. traditional Scottish method
- Flinders peat composition (swamp marsh herbs, tussocks, coastal heath, tea tree, melaleucas)
- Blend: 50 % Tasmanian peated malt + 50 % Scottish heavily peated malt
- Resulting flavour profile: earthy back‑palate + immediate smoky front

## Source Excerpts
- Like any strong flavour, sometimes you need to acclimatise.
- There are peated whisky drinkers out there who count PPM (phenolic parts per million, the measurement of smoky flavour in whisky), those drinkers are hardcore, and only want the smokiest drams.
- It spends much of it's time underwater, and provides a very interesting flavour.
- We are having a lot of fun experimenting with the flavours at our disposal.
- We wanted to see how the flavours of peat from either ends of the globe would work together, so for these batches of whisky, we combined half Tasmanian malt that we had peated on the island, with half Scottish heavily peated malt.
- The majority of the Flinders peat smoke flavour sits on the back palate, quite earthy, warm and aromatic.
