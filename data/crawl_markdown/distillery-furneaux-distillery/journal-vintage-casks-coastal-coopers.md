# Vintage Casks & Coastal Coopers

The article explains how Furneaux Distillery uses a variety of wooden barrels—ranging from virgin oak to Australian timbers and ex‑wine casks—to age whisky. It details the journey of ex‑solera Muscat casks from Campbells Wines in Rutherglen, their transport across Bass Strait, and the cooperage work undertaken on Flinders Island to refurbish them. The piece also highlights current whiskies that use similar barrels and invites readers to stay tuned for future releases.

---

## Key Points
- Australian law requires whisky to age in a wooden barrel for at least 2 years.
- Furneaux holds hundreds of oak barrels, some maturing until after 2030.
- Barrel type, timber, age, origin and previous contents influence flavour.
- Modern distilleries experiment with virgin oak, red gum, jarrah, wine, mezcal, rum casks.
- Ex‑solera Muscat casks from Campbells Wines (Rutherglen) were transported to Flinders Island for refurbishment.
- Cooperage involves disassembling, shaving staves, toasting and reassembling; toasted wood adds vanilla notes and charcoal filtering.
- Current whiskies: Sawyers Bay Single Malt (ex‑sherry & bourbon), Smoky Wedding (American oak ex‑bourbon), Sawyers Bay Peated Finish (second‑fill casks).

## Takeaway
Furneaux Distillery is actively sourcing and restoring unique barrels to create distinct whisky profiles, blending traditional cooperage with modern barrel experimentation.

- In the meantime, try our other whiskies, Sawyers Bay Single Malt matured in ex-sherry and bourbon, Smoky Wedding matured in American Oak ex-Bourbon casks, and our Sawyers Bay Peated Finish, matured in second fill casks for a lighter oak influence.
- Wine barrels such as Sherry and Port are also very popular; a slow evolution from a time when timber barrels were the only method of transporting liquid, often a barrel would arrive in Scotland full of Port or Sherry, then would be sold to a distillery once it was emptied.
- We have hundreds of oak barrels maturing in the bond store on the Furneaux Farm.
- The style of the barrel, its timber, its age and origin, if it held liquid previously, all of these factors will determine the flavour the barrel will impart to the whisky as it ages.
- They range from unused "virgin" oak to Australian timber like red gum or jarrah, wine barrels, mezcal barrels from Mexico, or rum barrels from the Caribbean.
- Historically, barrels used to mature bourbon have been prevalent due to the industry's law that bourbon can ONLY be matured in a virgin cask, so the secondary market is vast.
