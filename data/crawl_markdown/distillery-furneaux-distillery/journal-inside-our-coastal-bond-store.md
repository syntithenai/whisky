# Inside Our Coastal Bond Store
Furneaux Distillery has built a dunnage warehouse at Wickham to store its new‑make spirit for maturation. The open‑earth design, used by Scottish producers like Bruichladdich and Kilchoman, keeps barrels cool and humid, encouraging slow flavour development while limiting excessive oak influence.

## Key Points
- **New bond store**: dunnage warehouse at Wickham farm (Sawyers Bay).
- **Construction**: Built by John Riddle’s local builders.
- **Storage style**: Open‑earth floor vs. conventional racked concrete sheds.
- **Benefits**: Regulated temperature, high humidity, reduced evaporation, balanced oak influence.
- **Maturation range**: 2–20 years depending on barrel size.

## Source Excerpts
- For example, a fortified wine cask like sherry or port will give our whisky a rich and dark fruity flavour, whereas a bourbon cask will give it a lighter, caramel and toffee influence.
- This interaction is good for a whisky to develop flavour, but if the barrels are heating too aggressively, there will be an excess of timber contact, and the whisky will have developed a deep oaky flavour before it has had time to truly mature in the cask.
- A barrel is breathing during maturation; as the temperature increases the barrels will expand, drawing the spirit into the timber, and as they cool down and contract, the spirit will be expelled from the timber, drawing from it the compounds that make up a whisky’s flavour.
- So generally speaking – dark and cool is beneficial for a whisky’s slow maturation, drawing on cask influence while the whisky is breathing and developing a balance of flavour.
- All of this whisky needs to go into barrels for maturation, and depending on the size of the barrel, that can be anywhere from 2 years up to 20 years!
- A whisky gets a vast amount of its flavour from the barrel it’s sitting in.
