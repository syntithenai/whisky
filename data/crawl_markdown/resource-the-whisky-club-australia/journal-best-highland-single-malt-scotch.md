# Best Highland Single Malt Scotch
The Whisky Club Australia’s guide to the best Highland single‑malt whiskies of 2026 covers the region’s diversity, sub‑regions and key distilleries. It explains why Highland whiskies are typically heavier and maltier, highlights four regional styles (Northern, Southern, Coastal, Eastern), and lists exclusive club releases from Glenmorangie, Tomatin, Glendronach, Tullibardine, Glenglassaugh, Ardnamurchan, Loch Lomond, Dalmore, Ardmore, and Wolfburn. The article also includes FAQs on popularity, peat use, and entry‑level options.

---

## Key Takeaways
- **Highland diversity**: 4 sub‑regions + islands (Islay, Orkney, Skye, Mull, Arran). 
- **Flavor profile**: heavier, oilier, more robust than Lowland/Speyside. 
- **Exclusive club releases**: 13 yr Moscatel (Glenmorangie), 14 yr Rum‑cask (Tomatin), 2013 Oloroso (Glendronach), 11 yr Quinterris (Tullibardine), 12 yr PX (Glenglassaugh), 10 yr Ardnamurchan, 14 yr French Oak Limousin (Loch Lomond), 17 yr Luminary No. 3 (Dalmore), 8 yr Ardmore 2008, 13th cask Wolfburn.
- **FAQs**: Glenmorangie is most popular; Highland Park is technically a Highland malt; Speyside is a sub‑region but distinct in style; peat use varies.

## FAQs Summary
- **Most popular**: Glenmorangie (global). 
- **Highland Park**: technically Highland malt from Orkney. 
- **Speyside vs Highland**: Speyside lighter & fruitier; Highland heavier & malty. 
- **Peat use**: varies; notable peated distilleries include Ardnamurchan, Ardmore, Highland Park, Lagg, Talisker.
- **Entry‑level**: Glenmorangie, Loch Lomond, Glendronach, Clynelish.
